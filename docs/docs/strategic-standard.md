# The strategic standard

This is the standard every outcome's `strategicMove` must meet. It exists because the first draft of the model described outcomes at too low a level ("institutions re-earn relevance by showing up reliably and delivering value"), which is a truism, not a strategy. The standard forces the writing up to the level the project needs: each outcome should read as a decision a public-health leader could act on.

## The core idea

Treat each outcome not as scenario mood but as a **compressed strategic hypothesis**:

> Public health wins or loses here **IF** it does **X**, at **Y** venue, which is hard because **Z**.

## The two fields

- **`narrative`** = one sentence. The *world-state*: what the world looks like if this outcome happens. Consistent with the pole (positive = the good world, negative = the bad world, mixed = genuinely two-sided).
- **`strategicMove`** = 4 to 6 sentences carrying the strategy. It must name four things and end with a "The move:" line.

## The four things a strategic move must name

1. **Mechanism** — the actual move or vehicle. Never a truism ("deliver value", "build trust", "show up reliably"). The specific thing done.
2. **Venue / actor** — the concrete surface where it is won. Something you can point at: an AI assistant, a wearable, a pharmacy, a CHW, a Medicaid state plan, a procurement contract, a data standard, a school-entry requirement, a statute, a harm-reduction program, a community network.
3. **The hard part** — the cost, tradeoff, or thing given up. Strategy lives in the tension. A move with no cost is a wish.
4. **The implied move** — a final sentence starting "The move:" stating what a PHI should actually do.

## Two tests every strategic move must pass

- **Negation test.** Could a smart person argue the *opposite* mechanism? If the stated cause is something nobody would dispute (deliver value, build trust), it is a truism, not a claim. Rewrite until there is a real alternative you rejected.
- **So-what test.** Does it imply an action a PHI leader could take on Monday? If it does not change what anyone does, it is description, not strategy. Rewrite.

The altitude to aim for: **specific enough to argue with, general enough to be a strategy and not an anecdote.**

## The coherence thesis (use where it fits, do not force)

A recurring pattern runs through the strong outcomes, and it is the through-line of the whole project:

- The strongest **positive** move is usually some version of **"mandate to substrate"**: public health stops trying to persuade or mobilize a public it may have lost, and instead becomes the **funded steward of a commons**, the **trusted source others cite**, or the **setter of defaults and standards** (engineering the healthy choice into the food supply, the AI layer, coverage rules, procurement). It wins at the touchpoint, not the podium.
- The strongest **negative** paths are usually the **default that wins by inertia** because that steward/default move was never made: the market or the crisis cycle or the vendor lock-in takes the ground while public health arrives with opinions but no leverage.

Related idea used in the alignment field and worth keeping in view: for much of the disease burden (chronic, metabolic, behavioral) individual good and public good already coincide, so the job is to let self-interest do the work where it is *self-aligned*, *engineer* alignment where you can, and reserve scarce collective machinery for the genuinely divergent cases (contagion, the commons).

## Style rules

- **No em-dashes.** Use commas, colons, periods.
- `narrative`: one sentence. `strategicMove`: 4 to 6 sentences, ending "The move:".
- Do not change an outcome's `direction` or `alignment` when rewriting prose.
- Keep it public-health-specific and concrete.

## Worked examples (write at this level)

**Positive, engineered alignment — "Public stewardship of AI"**
- *World-state:* The data, rules, and guardrails for health AI are set in the public interest, not by whoever ships fastest.
- *Strategic move:* Public health stewards the shared substrate the models run on: an open, consented, representative health-data commons, published evaluation standards, and disclosure of training data and failure modes. The venue is the data-and-standards layer, upstream of any single app. What makes it real is aggregating buyer leverage, pooling procurement across health departments so vendors must meet public terms. The hard part: a commons has no revenue and no constituency once the crisis passes, so it dies without dedicated funding. The move: be the neutral steward and secure the financing a commons always lacks.

**Positive, needs collective action — "CHWs become permanent infrastructure"**
- *World-state:* Community health workers become permanent, financed infrastructure with real career ladders, not a rotating cast of grant-funded temps.
- *Strategic move:* The move is to make CHWs a standing part of the system: sustainable financing (Medicaid reimbursement, foundational funding), certification and career ladders, and integration into care teams. The venue is Medicaid policy, state certification, and payment models, the places that turn a grant line into a job. The hard part is that CHWs have been "the future" for decades and keep dying in the grant cycle because their financing is soft. The move: fight the CHW battle on financing and career structure, not on evidence, because what kills CHW programs is the funding model, not the proof they work.

**Negative, self-aligned — "Renting attention on others' platforms"**
- *World-state:* Public health only ever rents attention on platforms it does not control.
- *Strategic move:* The default, because building owned audience is hard and posting on existing platforms is easy. The engine is convenience plus the absence of product capacity. The hard part is that rented reach is fragile and adversarial: algorithm changes, deplatforming, and hostile amplification can erase or weaponize your presence. The move: reduce dependence on rented platforms by building owned channels and trusted-messenger networks, because a strategy that lives on someone else's platform is a strategy someone else controls.

## Before / after (why the standard exists)

The outcome that triggered this standard, still unwritten in the keystone:

- *Weak (rejected):* "By showing up reliably and delivering value in daily life, public institutions win back a place people actually use. They become a trusted default rather than a fallback." — This restates the outcome and offers a truism as the mechanism. Fails the negation test (nobody argues you win relevance by being unreliable) and the so-what test (implies no action).
- *At standard (direction to take):* name the mechanism (embed as infrastructure under the surfaces people already use), the venue (the AI assistant that cites you, the wearable/pharmacy data standard, the CHW at neighborhood scale), the hard part (ceding the visible front end to partners, being invisible infrastructure), and the move.
